#2. Content is King

Great content isn’t stumbled upon,
it’s carefully designed for a specific goal.

Source: GatherContent
Content has an undeniable effect on design.
Content provides the context.
Without it, a website is an empty shell and we're left with taking a stab in the dark.

If websites are essentially a vehicle for content delivery,
why do we disproportionately focus on visual design,
at the expense of the content?

Source: GatherContent
When we treat content as an afterthought we limit our ability to make good design decisions and our sites fail to achieve their goals.

"Design in the absence of content
is not design, it's decoration."

- Jeffery Zeldman. Founder, Happy Cog
So why do we postpone content and lead with design?
Doing this is like starting a fight with one arm tied behind your back.
Instead, let’s get both fists up — and always pair the design and content.

Obviously there's a place for beauty in a product.
But aesthetics aren’t the first thing that
we as web designers should tackle.

...
Let’s put it this way: Creating a website design without content is a guessing game. Period.

Content is king, and with it, we can provide a tool that actually helps clients advertise their product or services.

Design and content are equally important for success.
Giving them equal weight will dramatically increase the quality of your work.

#3. Get User-Centeric

It’s pretty likely that your client isn’t the main user of the website you are designing

...
A client’s natural inclination will be to give you their personal opinion on a design. This is reinforced because you ask them what THEY think of the design. Instead, we should ask them what their users will think of the design.

Even if they can clearly articulate their exact tastes and preferences, it’s beside the point because they are not the target audience.

Source: A List Apart
The fact is - It is not the client’s job to know exactly what their website should look like or how it should function. It’s OUR job.

They come to us to share ideas, to express concerns, likes, and dislikes...

But ultimately, our clients look to us in helping guide them to a solution.
What is User-Centered Design?

Typically, our sites are designed from a business perspective, with consideration for users added in later.

Instead, UCD ensures a process focused on what users need...

User-Centred Design (UCD) ensures a process focused on what users need before balancing this with technical and business requirements.

Source: Experience UX

UCD Process

  1. Know the User
  2. Define the Problem
  3. Research the Competition
  4. Propose the Solution
  5. Test the Outcome
  6. Iterate the Design

Ask a few questions about who the end user is and what their needs are to better understand the target audience you are actually designing for.

Source: A List Apart

User-centered Questions

  • Who exactly do you anticipate will be using this website?
  • What will they accomplish by using this website?
  • What are their pain points?
  • What problem is this website solving for them?

Two Mindsets

    Fixed Mindset - Intelligence is Static

    Leads to a desire to look smart and a tendency to...

  • Avoid challenges
  • Give up easily
  • See effort as fruitless or worse
  • Ignore useful negative feedback
  • Feel threatened by the success of others

    • As a result, they may plateau early and
    achieve less than their full potential

    Growth Mindset - Intelligence Can be Developed

    Leads to a desire to learn and a tendency to...

  • Embrace challenges
  • Persist in the face of setbacks
  • See effort as the path to mastery
  • Learn from criticism
  • Find lessons and inspiration in the success of others

    • As a result, they reach even higher
